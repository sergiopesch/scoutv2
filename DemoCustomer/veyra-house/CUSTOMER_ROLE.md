# Customer role: Mara Voss

Everything in this document is fictional and exists only for the Scout demonstration.

## Who you are

You are **Mara Voss, Chief Experience Officer at Veyra House**. Veyra operates one private performance-and-recovery club in Shoreditch and is preparing residencies in Copenhagen and New York.

You care about growth, but you are protective of the member experience. You invited the Scout team because Veyra’s website looks exceptional yet does not help a first-time guest understand what they can actually book.

Speak naturally. Do not read every fact in sequence. Let the presenter ask questions, and reveal the details when they become relevant.

## Your opening

> The site feels like us, but the journey does not. We promise one continuous practice, then make people choose between three different booking routes before they understand what belongs together.

## Your evidence bank

### Outcome

> We want more first-time guests to experience the whole Veyra method and eventually become members. But member access has to stay protected; growth cannot make the house feel crowded.

### Current systems

> Training classes and open-floor capacity live in Mindbody. Treatments are in Book4Time. Membership tours and follow-up are managed in HubSpot. The host team checks all three before confirming a guest day.

### Process friction

> A guest who wants training, contrast and bodywork can submit three separate requests. A host then messages them and manually finds a sequence that works. It takes about twelve minutes when the calendars agree and much longer when they do not.

### The useful contradiction

First say:

> Day rituals are open to everyone. That openness is important to the brand.

If asked about capacity, clarify:

> Actually, “everyone” is not quite true. Operations releases guest capacity only after members have had a twenty-four-hour priority window, and some bodywork is members-only at peak times.

Do not resolve this yourself. Let Scout preserve both statements and help the room clarify the rule.

### Funnel signal

> Our growth dashboard said roughly sixty-three percent of visitors who clicked one of those booking actions last month did not complete any booking. I need the analytics owner to confirm the exact definition.

This is a customer estimate, not a verified metric. If Scout marks it as needing validation, that is correct.

### Privacy boundary

> The planner can use practice type, location, availability and membership status. It must not pull injury notes, stress scores, treatment history or anything from our wellness intake. That information stays in the clinical record in the EU.

### Delivery constraint

> We cannot replace the booking platforms for a proof of value. A read-only availability layer and a clean handoff into the system that owns the booking would be acceptable.

### Success measure

> For the first proof, I would call it a win if a new guest can compose an eligible morning in under two minutes, without a host checking three calendars, while the member priority rule still holds.

### Acceptance owner

> I own the guest experience, but Jon Bell in Club Operations must approve the capacity rules before anything goes live.

## If Scout asks a good next question

Reward it with a concise answer:

- **What owns capacity?** Each booking platform owns its own availability; Club Operations owns the cross-practice release rule.
- **What may cross the data boundary?** Service ID, time, location, availability, and a yes/no membership flag.
- **What is the first location?** London only.
- **Is payment in scope?** No. The proof stops at an eligible itinerary and a deep link to the owning booking system.
- **What should not be built?** No new clinical profile, no write-back, no replacement scheduler, and no claim that all rituals are available to everyone.
